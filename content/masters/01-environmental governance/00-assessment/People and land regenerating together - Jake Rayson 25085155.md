# People and land regenerating together
## Are Result Based Schemes a step toward flourishing communities in Wales?
> Wales is now one of the most nature-depleted countries on Earth… significant and ongoing changes in the way we manage our land for agriculture, and the effects of climate change, are having the biggest impacts on our wildlife… Wales’ biodiversity and wider environment continues to decline and degrade
> *State of Nature Wales 2023*

I propose that the underlying cause of this decline is capitalism, and will examine the idea of Results Based Payment Schemes as an envrionmental governance intervention that could provide a stepping stone toward an egalitarian food system decoupled from deleterious market forces, whilst enhancing biodiversity and increasing resilience to climate shocks.

Capitalism is an economic system based on the primacy of private property used for the pursuit of profit and the accumulation of capital by the commodification of everything, to the exclusion of everything else.

Industrial agriculture is a capitalist venture, leading to “changes in land use arising from a push to increase yields as much as possible so as to maximise profit margins… capitalism is the cause of the Yellowhammer’s decline” (Dean, 2021), where many of the fields of grass are a “factory floor – ploughed, reseeded, fertilised and sprayed with herbicide to maximise productivity” (Plantlife 2014). Simultaneously, agricultural abandonment also threatens ecosystems (Stoate et al. 2009).

But the ultimate hidden truth of the world is that it doesn’t have to be this way (Graeber 2024). Indeed, it hasn’t been for the vast majority of human existence, and human activity is not inherently anti-nature.

From the seminal paper *When culture meets biodiversity : The case of the wooded meadow*, “Neither in any other community of Western Europe, nor anywhere in the tropics, does the number of vascular plant species within a few square meters reach the numbers counted on the Estonian wooded meadows.” (Kull et al., 2003, p. 76). 

And critically “Biodiversity of the meadow ecosystems is connected to human use and it declines significantly if this use ceases.” (Kull et al., 2003, p. 89). The point here is that the right kind and level of human intervention in landscapes over time can **increase** biodiversity, counter to the pessimism of neo-Malthusians (Ioris, 2025, p. 15). See also Anderson 2005 and Graves 2024 for a cornucopia of evidence. This is not a technical issue, we know *how* to increase biodiversity; this is an economic, cultural and ultimately political issue.

There is a lot of work to do though. 90% of Welsh land is used for agriculture, mostly for sheep and cattle (Neil 2022), and 50% of that land is “improved” grassland dominated by a monoculture of Rye-grass (Blackstock 2010), leading to a significant loss of biodiversity (Malisch et al. 2024).

Because the way that agricultural land is managed has been identified as *the* most significant factor in driving species population changes (Burns et al. 2016), and the fact that agriculture globally is responsible for up to 37% of greenhouse gas emissions (SAPEA 2020), agricultural land management needs to be front and centre of nature recovery efforts. As a counter-point, there does need to be a way to “make nature pay“ so that communities engaged in land management can *afford* to do the right thing.
### Result Based Schemes
In Europe, the “scientific consensus… is that agri-environment measures have provided only marginal biodiversity gains” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p.32), so there have been increasing calls “for the integration of an ecosystem service approach into agri-environment measures and a payment structure based on the delivery of results (results-oriented)”  (Ibid., p. 26)

“Result-based agri-environment schemes pay land managers not for performing specific management actions (such as mowing on set dates), but for achieving set environmental outcomes, such as a species rich grassland or the promotion of an endangered species” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 36), although the overwhelming majority of agri-environment schemes remain action-based (Ibid., p. 30).

Result Based Schemes have been adopted across Europe, with Ireland leading the way in the number of schemes and the area covered, and also in the analysis, specifically the anthology *Farming for Nature : The Role of Results-Based Payments* (O'Rourke et al., 2020).

Wales has just, in January 2026, commenced a new post-Brexit agri-environment scheme, the Sustainable Farming Scheme (Welsh Govt. 2026), which is action- based, as “Farmers entering the SFS must commit to 12 “Universal Actions” in return for entry-level payments.” (Case, 2026), with a phasing out of payments from the Basic Payment Scheme (Ibid.).

The biodviersity results of Result Based Schemes are very encouraging, “This collection of case studies provides proof-of-practice that results-based approaches can be successfully designed and implemented to achieve biodiversity objectives in high nature value farmland.” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 165) However, the phrase “high nature value farmland“ does call for some attention: it is farmland which *tends* to be not so productive and semi-natural habitats, rather than high yield areas. (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 38) The question whether Result Based Schemes can be applied to high yield areas is essentially an economic question.

Even though Result Based Schemes are a step toward the decommodification of food, as it becomes an additional income for the farmer, what is interesting is that they are built upon the inherently capitalist ecosystem services approach. This “strives to commodify environmental public goods in an attempt to counteract market failure, which currently classifies them simply as free ‘externalities’” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 24), couched in the same language of finance as The Dasgupta Review (Dasgupta, 2024). I would argue that “Introducing natural capital into national accounting systems” (Ibid., p. 4) does nothing to deter short-term profit seeking or capital accumulation, an example being a carbon trading investment firm with holdings in Ceredigion, whose “profit-driven approach “raised questions about the long-term commitment to restoring nature, rather than treating land as another investible commodity”” (Carrell and editor, 2025) according to the policy director at Community Land Scotland.

Nevertheless, “Result-oriented Agri-Environmental Schemes require a cultural change in the way farmers view the environment and engage with policy on the ground, along with a governance structure that is participatory and capable of adaptive management” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 14).

This gets to the heart of the matter, as it opens the door for a change in the *substance* of governance by introducing the necessary idea of a ‘Locally Led Results Based Approach’  “because in the delivery of LLRBA and nature friendly farming, one needs to build links between farming systems and the social and economic lives of the communities embedded in these places.” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 15). In the words of Carl Sauer, “Culture is the agent, the natural area is the medium, the cultural landscape the result” (Denevan and Mathewson, 2009, p. 15), and it is only through the engagement of local expertise and locally vested interests that we can cultivate and regenerate beneficial long-term relationships between the people in their land. Wooded meadows can only flourish when they are supported by culture.

As well as a partial decommodification of food and the requirement for a cultural change toward local governance, Result Based Schemes also highlight the importance of education and awareness, both in terms of the knowledge of local landscapes and management, and the skills required for identifying the indicator species underpinning the payment system. “Without exception, all of the case studies considered training and education to be a crucial success factor.” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 283). This can key into a broader celebration and regeneration of Welsh cultures, such as *Geiriau Diflanedig—The Lost Words* (Amgueddfa Cymru 2023), a project highlighting the words of the natural world being lost from our dictionaries and everyday use, which “explores the relationship between language and the living world, and of nature’s power to spark the imagination” (Ibid.).
### Improvements
A useful analogy in comparing Action Based Schemes with Result Based Schemes is found in computer science. There is a distinction between imperative programming, which dictates explicit steps, and declarative programming, which declares *what* the desired outcome should be and leaves the implementation to the compiler.

By their declarative nature, Result Based Schemes already start to shift the balance of power in environmental governance away from the centre, through the mechanism of a Locally Led Results Based Approach, leading to greater community engagement: “The building of social capital – networks, trust, information sharing, along with acquiring new skills, knowledge and awareness - is vital in the longterm change in behaviour, attitudes and values required for the delivery of ecosystem services.” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 294)

The effectiveness of Result Based Schemes could arguably be improved by using them in **conjunction** with other, cross-departmental policy tools, which could help magnify the benefits of local, community-led climate resilience actions.

For example, the Food Sense Wales pilot project Welsh Veg in Schools “has been addressing how sustainable local veg production can supply into procurement” and “providing evidence on the role organic veg production has to play in ensuring horticulture is a viable career, improving food security and health, increasing biodiversity, reducing carbon emissions, mitigating climate change and building resilience” (Wheeler, 2025, p. 3).

Since 2024, free school meals are available in primary schools (Welsh Govt. 2024) and contracting authorities “must seek to improve the economic, social, environmental and cultural well-being of its area by carrying out public procurement in a socially responsible way” (Wheeler, 2025, p. 7).

A Results Based Scheme in this instance could provide a diversification of income for the farms supplying the Welsh Veg in Schools project.

Another example could be to integrate a ”community enterprise agroecology” project on a county farm such as the one recently announced in Lampeter (*Ceredigion County Council* 2025), where grant-funded infrastructure would establish land-based, long-term sustainable livelihoods such as coppicing or silvopasture, in combination with wildlife workshops with local schools. This would be a permeable ecological and economic enclave, offering enough protection from fickle markets for the businesses and long-term infrastructure to get established.

Another policy tool is Universal Basic Income. A conservation basic income has been proposed by convivial conservationists (Fletcher and Büscher 2020) and a food producers basic income by Basic Income 4 Farmers (Poulton et al. 2024). Farmers’ behaviour is typically risk averse (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 286), and a Universal Basic Income could encourage a higher take-up if used in conjunction with a Results Based Scheme. 
### Efficacy and control
With the urgency of the climate crisis, there are questions about the efficacy of  decentralised, locally-led action and whether they can be scaled up in time “a key challenge is: how to upscale locally-led results based approaches? This is probably one of the most important challenges that need to be resolved if agri-environmental conservation efforts are to be implemented at a pace and scale that matches the corresponding threats.” (O'Rourke et al., 2020, p. 297)

I would argue that the *underlying* driver of species extinction and habitat loss on agricultural land is the commodification of food, and this is what needs to be addressed. By removing the financial incentives to maximise the return on animal farming, and replacing them with a mixture of Result Based Schemes, a government procurement market for local food growers and Universal Basic Income, then you would find the scale and speed at a local level across Wales.

Ill-advised, sweeping and centralised national environmental policies can cause damaging effects at a pace and a scale; a recent example is the Great British Insulation Scheme, which has damaged 98% of 23,000 homes clad with external wall insulation (Conway 2025).

Also, the community engagement and involvement is *essential* for the success of the projects. Action has to be *grounded* in place as “there are no places in the abstract” (with apologies to Erik Swyngedouw).
### Conclusion
Species extinction and habitat loss in Wales is caused by changes in predominantly agricultural land use and the effects of climate change. Both of these in part are caused by the commodification of food and the pursuit of profit.

Result Based Schemes for agriculture have a positive effect on biodiversity, particularly compared to traditional Action Based Schemes. Of specific interest is their local and decentralised nature and the level of community participation, combined with the partial decommodification of food.

To capitalise on and boost these strengths, Result Based Schemes can work in collaboration with other environmental and social interventions such as public sector local food procurement, Universal Basic Income and sustainable livelihood and community infrastructure projects. By focussing on the regeneration of people with the land, together, we can prepare the conditions for necessary social change.

> Social change will not come to us like an avalanche down the mountain. Social change will come through seeds growing in well prepared soil — and it is we, like the earthworms, who prepare the soil.

(Franklin, 2004, p. 73)
