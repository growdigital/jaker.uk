## Connecting Children With Food, Farming, and Nature  
Rephrasing the title as question:
> How do we connect children with nature-positive food and farming?

There is a nature and climate emergency which is having a dramatic impact on food systems globally. Mass extinction is caused by habitat loss and climate change, in large part driven by industrial agricultural food systems.

For me, the *purpose* of the project should be to engage children with how their food is grown and equip them with critical thinking skills so that they can ask questions about whether a food system is nature-positive.

To that end, **wildlife ID skills** are essential. For example, at certain times of the year, a low-input, semi-natural wild flower meadow might superficially look a monoculture of perennial rye-grass. But counting plant and invertebrate species in a square metre tells a different story.

As I mentioned before, I think a 3 zone approach would be useful:
1. **Schools** - children growing perennial native herbs in custom habitat beds, in conjunction with outdoor lessons plans around wildlife ID, habitat creation, and nature-positive food growing that key-in to national curriculum. This space provides a daily opportunity for engagement.
2. **Locality** - build a relationship between schools and a local growing space within walking distance. Community gardens and allotments are ideal candidates, as they are generally nature-positive. There are [28 allotments in Cardiff](https://www.outdoorcardiff.com/allotments/?cn-reloaded=1). Community orchards would also be good but I don't know if there are any. These spaces provide a weekly opportunity for out-of-school visits.
3. **Farms** - armed with the knowledge and skills from their school environment and allotment engagement, the childrens' visits to farms would be greatly enriched, and enable them to question the farming practices they encounter.

And then the answers to research questions can be organised around this framework.

Food Policy Alliance Cymru recommends that ==every child in Wales should leave school  Food Literate== — able to understand where food comes from, how it’s grown, and how it  shapes health, culture, and the environment.

In Cardiff, the most urban and densely populated area in Wales, this vision requires  creative thinking. ==Ensuring that every child has the opportunity to visit== a farm, grower, or  ***nature‑friendly*** producer — and ==access meaningful growing spaces in school== — could  ***transform food education and wellbeing***. (My emphasis)
### Research Questions
1. What do farmers, growers, and producers need (training, funding, infrastructure,  safeguarding processes) to confidently and sustainably host school visits?  
2. What do schools need (curriculum links, transport solutions, staff capacity,  partnerships) to embed regular farm or grower visits into the learning journey?  
3. What opportunities exist in and around Cardiff for ==nature‑ and climate‑friendly  farm visits==, and what funding or grant mechanisms could make universal access  achievable?  
4. How could Cardiff design a scalable model that ensures equitable access for  children across all neighbourhoods?

This project could include mapping local assets, interviewing educators and producers,  reviewing funding opportunities, and designing practical models for implementation.
